## Thematic Game Design

Rival Hearts is a two-player cooperative game about romance, secrecy, and trust inside a watchful royal court. One player is the Suitor, who must send a private letter. The other is the Confidant, who knows the truth of the court but cannot speak freely. A hidden Rival is watching one route each round. The players win by earning 3 Hearts before 3 Rumours expose the affair.

The game is designed around a simple question: how much meaning can two players build from a tiny symbolic language? The Confidant knows three hidden facts each round: the recipient's mood, the danger route, and the Rival route. They may place exactly two signals on routes or tones using Heart, Thorn, Clock, and Mask. They also choose one secret action: Clear Danger, Delay Rival, or Strengthen Letter. The Suitor sees only the routes, tones, and signals, then chooses one route and one tone.

The round resolves with one token. If the Suitor chose the danger route and Clear Danger was not used, the players gain 1 Rumour. Otherwise, if the Suitor chose the Rival route, Delay Rival was not used, and the tone does not match the mood, the players gain 1 Rumour. In all other cases, they gain 1 Heart. Strengthen Letter makes any tone count as matching the mood.

This design intentionally removes card hands, action points, complex travel math, Rival traits, and question tokens from active play. The earlier version had strong atmosphere but too many rules competing for attention. The redesigned version keeps the strongest novelty: one player has the truth, but can only communicate through symbols. The depth now comes from interpretation rather than rule volume.

The three routes create compact strategic variety. The Fast Route suggests urgency, the Safe Route suggests public caution, and the Secret Route suggests intimacy and deception. The three tones keep the romance premise central: Tender, Bold, and Honest are not abstract stats, but emotional approaches to the letter. The four signals are deliberately open. Heart can mean "good choice" or "tone fits". Thorn can mean danger. Clock can mean speed or timing. Mask can mean deception or the Rival. Because signals have no automatic effect, their meaning depends on player trust and context.

This alignment between theme and mechanics supports meaningful play. Salen and Zimmerman argue that meaningful play emerges when actions have discernible and integrated outcomes (2003). Rival Hearts makes outcomes discernible through a reveal table showing mood, danger route, Rival route, action, Suitor choice, and the reason for the token. It is integrated because every choice relates to the same fiction: secret romance under surveillance. The Suitor's uncertainty is not a failure of information design. It is the emotional experience of trusting a confidant in a hostile court.

The main balance decision is that the Confidant can protect only one problem each round. Clear Danger does not stop the Rival. Delay Rival does not stop danger. Strengthen Letter does not clear a route. This prevents the Confidant from solving the puzzle alone and keeps the Suitor's interpretation important. It also avoids repetitive outcomes, because hidden facts can overlap in different ways. A route can be dangerous, watched by the Rival, both, or neither. A signal can point to a route, a tone, or a relationship between them.

The player experience should feel tense but readable. A beginner can learn the rules in a few minutes: two roles, three routes, three tones, four signals, one action, one token. After that, the interesting play is social. Players start asking what a symbol meant, whether the obvious warning was bait, and whether their partner expected them to read one signal literally or relationally. The best moment is not simply winning. It is losing and realising, "That is what you meant."
